\title{\Large Neuroinformatics CS-GY 9923 Project 3: Cell Segmentation and Spatial Cell-Type Classification on a Whole-Brain MERFISH Atlas
\newline}
\author{Chetan Goel, cg4652@nyu.edu}

\date{}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Project~3 contains two Kaggle tracks \cite{lu2026phase1, lu2026phase2} on a whole-brain MERFISH atlas: Phase~1 cell segmentation, and Phase~2 four-level Allen Brain Cell Atlas classification. A 28-epoch StarDist2D fine-tune on DAPI reached Phase~1 Kaggle ARI $\mathbf{0.7627}$, improving over the $0.632$ baseline. For Phase~2, a 5-way kNN ensemble using a Phase-1 Cellpose segmentation and $\sim$29{,}000 matched-source AWS Zhuang-ABCA-4 cells reached Kaggle ARI $\mathbf{0.5675}$, improving over the $0.351$ baseline. The main lessons in our setup were that validation ARI was a poor cross-family proxy for Kaggle ARI, and that the foreground/background gate dominated Phase~2 performance under heavy background imbalance.
\end{abstract}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}

We treat the MERFISH \cite{chen2015spatially} pipeline as two coupled inference problems: segmenting cells from DAPI/polyT staining channels so that decoded mRNA spots can be assigned to cells, and classifying each cell into the Allen Brain Cell Atlas hierarchy (\emph{class} $\supset$ \emph{subclass} $\supset$ \emph{supertype} $\supset$ \emph{cluster}). Segmentation errors propagate into classification: a missed cell becomes background spots, a merged pair becomes a chimeric expression vector.

Across the two phases we converge on two project-specific findings: (i) on instance-level non-differentiable metrics, validation ARI is not a reliable cross-family proxy for held-out Kaggle ARI; and (ii) on a metric with extreme class imbalance ($\sim$83\% \texttt{background} spots in Phase~2), the binary foreground/background gate dominates the score and must be tuned independently of the multi-class classifier. A third operational finding --- that matched-source labeled cells from the AWS Allen Brain Cell Atlas mirror (Zhuang-ABCA-4.001, byte-equivalent to the competition source session) lift Phase~2 Kaggle by more than the cumulative gain from $\sim$30 hand-tuned classifier experiments --- motivates the data section.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Data}

We use the competition releases for both phases as provided: Phase~1 has 40 train and 4 test FOVs, scored by per-FOV ARI on the spot-to-instance partition; Phase~2 has 60 train and 10 test FOVs, scored by the mean of per-(FOV, level) ARI over 4 hierarchy levels. The Cellpose-pretrained baseline scores Kaggle ARI $0.632$ in Phase~1; the Cellpose+kNN baseline scores mean ARI $0.351$ in Phase~2, essentially flat across levels (class $0.347$, subclass $0.352$, supertype $0.352$, cluster $0.351$). The Phase-2 cell-type distribution is long-tailed (11/37/62/83 values across levels, rare classes down to $\sim$15 cells), and $\sim$83\% of test spots carry the \texttt{background} label --- so as a first-order heuristic, a gain on the binary gate (which applies to all spots) is worth roughly $5\times$ a gain on the multi-class classifier (which only sees the $17\%$ foreground); ARI is not strictly per-spot accuracy, but the asymmetry holds empirically.

We augmented the Phase-2 labeled pool with $\sim$29{,}000 cells from the AWS Allen Brain Cell Atlas mirror \texttt{Zhuang-ABCA-4.001}. This section of the AWS mirror originates from the same BIL session (\texttt{220912\_wb3\_sa2\_2\_5z18R\_merfish5}) that produced the competition tiles. We verified the join on the subset of cell labels overlapping the competition train tiles: the 4-level Allen taxonomy strings matched exactly cell-for-cell, and the corresponding log-expression vectors agreed to within floating-point quantization, indicating the AWS mirror is the same Allen-pipeline output applied to the same imaging session. The $\sim$29{,}000 augmenting cells fall \emph{outside} the competition train and test tiles, so this is matched-source labeled augmentation rather than test-set leakage. We additionally prepared but did not use a cross-animal AWS pool (\texttt{aws\_mouse3}, 32K cells), which regressed Kaggle when included; matched-source data was the binding constraint, not data volume.

\section{Phase 1: Cell Segmentation}
\label{sec:phase1}

\subsection{Methods}
\label{subsec:why-stardist}

The task: assign each decoded mRNA spot to either a cell instance or \texttt{background}. We compared three segmentation families on a max-projection DAPI image (with polyT and a Gaussian-smoothed spot-density channel as optional extras): Cellpose v4 \cite{stringer2021cellpose} (U-Net++ with flow-field decoder), StarDist2D \cite{schmidt2018cell} (U-Net with a 32-ray star-convex polygon decoder), and the MEDIAR \cite{lee2022mediar} foundation model as a zero-shot baseline. Eighteen training variants in total: three Cellpose backbones ($\texttt{cyto2}, \texttt{cyto3}, \texttt{nuclei}$) crossed with augmentation / cosine-schedule / warmup variants, plus a 28-epoch StarDist fine-tune of \texttt{2D\_versatile\_fluo} on DAPI alone.

The submitted StarDist had only the 14\textsuperscript{th}-best validation ARI, but won on Kaggle (Table~\ref{tab:phase1}). The mechanism: ARI is computed on hard instance partitions, so a model whose pixel disagreements concentrate at cell boundaries can have very different ARI from one whose disagreements are interior --- pixel-level loss is a poor proxy. StarDist's star-convex prior hard-codes the compact-elliptical morphology of MERFISH nuclei, restricting the hypothesis space and acting as a strong regularizer. Cellpose's expressive flow field instead overfits to polygon noise in the training ground truth (which is itself Cellpose-2.0-generated). Confirming evidence: extending StarDist to 180 epochs raised val by $+0.021$ but lowered Kaggle by $-0.021$ --- the regularizer being gradually overridden. Figure~\ref{fig:inductive-bias} contrasts the two morphologies on the same crop.

\subsection{What didn't work}
\label{subsec:tta-failure}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{4-way TTA} ($\Delta = -0.017$). Averaging soft probabilities before non-max suppression produces fatter probability maps, which makes NMS merge adjacent cells; ARI is exquisitely sensitive to merges. Ensembling must happen at the hard-instance level for ARI.
    \item \textbf{Nearest-mask spot dilation} ($\Delta = -0.26$). Assigning extracellular spots to the nearest mask within $r=5$\,px transferred correctly-rejected \texttt{background} spots into the wrong instance.
    \item \textbf{Multi-$z$ stitching} and \textbf{per-$z$ samples}. Both regressed; the metric reads a 2D partition per FOV, so 3D structure adds variance.
    \item \textbf{Longer StarDist training} (180 epochs): $+0.021$ val, $-0.021$ Kaggle --- the regularizer being overridden, as predicted.
\end{itemize}

\section{Phase 2: Spatial Cell-Type Classification}
\label{sec:phase2}

\subsection{Pipeline}

Given a Phase-1 segmentation $S$ assigning each spot to a cell or \texttt{background}, we form a per-cell gene-count vector $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{1147}$ (log1p-normalized), then assign each spot four labels --- class, subclass, supertype, cluster (11/37/62/83 Allen taxonomy values plus \texttt{background}; the per-level ARI includes \texttt{background} spots). The pipeline has three modules: (i) segmentation (we carry over the Phase-1 Cellpose \texttt{nuclei\_cosine\_ep125} checkpoint, $(\texttt{cellprob}, \texttt{flow}) = (-0.5, 0.4)$); (ii) a binary background gate $g(s)$ calibrated to the 83\% prior; (iii) four parallel distance-weighted kNN classifiers ($k=5$, cosine metric) fit on the union of phase-2 train and AWS-augmented cells. The Kaggle metric is the mean of ARI over (FOV, level) pairs.

\textit{Why Cellpose for Phase 2 rather than the Phase-1 Kaggle-best StarDist?} The Phase-2 pipeline aggregates spots-inside-mask into a per-cell expression vector, so the relevant property is \emph{instance completeness} (does the mask capture the cytoplasmic-radius spots that vote for the cell's identity?), not boundary accuracy. StarDist's star-convex polygons are systematically smaller than the underlying cell body and miss peripheral spots; the more permissive Cellpose nuclei masks empirically aggregate cleaner per-cell expression vectors at the kNN's input. Three mixed Phase-2 submissions that used StarDist as one voter all regressed Kaggle (PQR-V7$+$StarDist 3-way scored $0.4979$ vs $0.5421$ for the all-Cellpose 4-way), confirming the empirical choice.

\subsection{What worked}

\textbf{Matched-pool augmentation was the largest single lift.} The AWS Allen Brain Cell Atlas mirror \texttt{Zhuang-ABCA-4.001} is the Allen Atlas pipeline output for the same BIL session that produced the competition tiles (verified cell-for-cell on the labels overlapping the competition train tiles). Adding $\sim$29{,}000 of its labelled cells to the training pool (a $5.5\times$ increase) lifted Kaggle from $0.5346$ to $0.5593$ ($\Delta = +0.025$) --- more than the cumulative gain from $\sim$30 hand-tuned classifier experiments combined. Because the augmenting cells share the same animal, session, panel, and labelling pipeline as Phase-2 train, there is no systematic distribution shift to bridge --- this is matched-pool augmentation, not transfer learning.

\textbf{kNN out-performed everything more expressive} (RandomForest, ExtraTrees, HGB, MLP-128, PCA$\to$RF, scANVI \cite{xu2021scanvi}, CellTypist \cite{dominguez2022celltypist}, XGBoost \cite{chen2016xgboost}). The rarest cluster-level classes have $\sim$15 cells, which collapses RandomForest split statistics (HGB scored $0.2431$ at cluster level). kNN's lazy retrieval has the right inductive bias: rare classes are correctly retrieved iff a test cell genuinely resembles a labeled one. Cosine distance on log1p expression matches single-cell-RNA-seq standard practice and is approximately scale-invariant to total RNA capture.

\textbf{Decoupling the gate from the classifier.} As a first-order intuition, a 10-point gain on a binary gate that touches all 83\% background spots should outweigh a 10-point gain on the 17\%-foreground multi-class classifier by roughly the prior ratio. ARI is not exactly per-spot accuracy and the formal decomposition is more subtle, but empirically the asymmetry held: our gate combines three signals --- $S$ assigns the spot to background; the cell's nearest classifier neighbour has cosine distance above $\tau$; the cell has fewer than $m$ spots total --- and tuning $(\tau, m)$ independently of the classifier (on the validation prior alone) was the single largest hand-tuned lift.

\textbf{Ensembling in classifier space, not segmentation space.} The final submission is a 5-way majority vote over kNN classifiers with different $(k, \text{metric}, \text{neighbour-weighting}, \tau)$, all reading the same segmentation. Kaggle: $0.5675$, $+0.0082$ over the best single voter. Every cross-segmentation voter we tried regressed the ensemble: two classifiers on different segmentations receive different per-cell features for the same underlying cells, and their errors are spatially correlated. Commit to the strongest segmentation, ensemble in classifier space.

\subsection{What didn't work}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cellpose-SAM fine-tunes as the segmentation backbone}. Phase-2-specific cpsam fine-tunes all regressed below the Phase-1 carryover; cpsam-zeroshot$+$RF500 collapsed to Kaggle $0.3378$, below the course baseline. Same regularization argument as Phase~1: more expressive models overfit at this data scale.
    \item \textbf{HGB at cluster level}. 200-tree HGB scored $0.2431$ locally on 83 cluster classes with 30+ rare classes ($<$50 cells) --- late-tree splits become noise-dominated.
    \item \textbf{Cross-animal AWS-augmentation}. Adding the \texttt{aws\_mouse3} pool (32K cells, different animal) regressed the ensemble. Matched-source data is the binding constraint.
    \item \textbf{Voting across classifier families} (Logreg/RF/HGB added to kNN). Cross-family errors concentrate on the gate, where different classifiers calibrate $\tau$ very differently --- the ensemble's average $\tau$ is worse than any individual.
    \item \textbf{The autoresearch agent loop} (\texttt{phase2/autoresearch/}). 23 iterations converged to a local-best ($0.5840$ val) that scored only $0.4881$ on Kaggle: the loop had overfit $\tau$ on the 5-FOV val set within $\sim$10 iterations. We froze it and switched to hand-tuned ensembling.
    \item \textbf{Hierarchy-aware structured classifier} (scaffolded, not finished). Expected to help on finer levels but minimal on the gate-dominated score.
\end{itemize}

\section{Ablation Summary}

The largest gains came from three sources: segmentation quality (Cellpose nuclei fine-tune over the pretrained baseline; $+0.13$ Phase~1 Kaggle), matched-source labeled-cell augmentation (AWS Zhuang-ABCA-4.001; $+0.025$ Phase~2 Kaggle), and classifier-space ensembling (5-way kNN majority vote; $+0.008$ Phase~2 Kaggle). In contrast, more expressive segmentation backbones (Cellpose-SAM fine-tunes), cross-segmentation ensemble voting, and the automated gate-search loop all produced \emph{worse} Kaggle generalization despite sometimes improving local validation ARI. The unifying theme is that on a non-differentiable instance-level metric with extreme class imbalance, well-matched data and constrained inductive bias beat additional model capacity at the scales we worked at.

\section{Conclusions}

\subsection{Phase 1: Key Findings}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inductive bias substitutes for training data on small medical-imaging datasets.} A 28-epoch StarDist fine-tune beat every Cellpose v4 variant on Kaggle ($0.7627$ vs.\ $0.7588$), despite ranking 14\textsuperscript{th} of 18 trained variants on validation. The star-convex prior restricts the hypothesis space to compact convex blobs --- matching MERFISH nuclear morphology --- acting as a regularizer that suppresses overfitting to training polygon irregularities. Confirming evidence: extending StarDist to 180 epochs raised val by $+0.021$ but \emph{lowered} Kaggle by $-0.021$.

    \item \textbf{Validation ARI was not a cross-family Kaggle proxy in our setup.} On instance-level non-differentiable metrics, validation appears reliable \emph{within} a family (shared overfitting dynamics) but unreliable \emph{across} families (different inductive biases produce different val-to-Kaggle gaps). The submitted StarDist's $14$\textsuperscript{th} val ARI / $1$\textsuperscript{st} Kaggle ARI inversion is the cleanest demonstration of this principle in our experiments; we have not tried to characterize how broadly it generalizes.

    \item \textbf{Soft-probability averaging is incompatible with instance-level metrics.} TTA dropped Kaggle from $0.7627$ to $0.7461$. Averaging soft probabilities before non-linear NMS produces masks that are not the ARI-mean of the averaged inputs. For ARI, ensembling must happen at the hard-instance level (e.g.\ majority voting on partitions), not at the probability level.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Phase 2: Key Findings}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Matched-pool augmentation dominates modelling choices once segmentation is sufficient.} Adding 29{,}000 AWS Zhuang-ABCA-4.001 cells lifted Kaggle from $0.5346$ to $0.5593$ ($\Delta = +0.025$) --- more than the cumulative gain from $\sim$30 hand-tuned classifier and ensemble experiments combined. This is not transfer learning: same animal, session, panel, and Allen-pipeline labels. The lesson for spatial transcriptomics: identify matched-source labeled corpora first; treat modelling as constrained by the resulting pool size.

    \item \textbf{The binary gate has $\sim$$4.9\times$ the leverage of the multi-class classifier on this metric.} With 83\% \texttt{background}, a 10-point gate-accuracy improvement scales score by $0.10$; the same improvement on the 17\% foreground scales score by $0.017$. We decoupled gate from classifier and tuned $(\tau, m)$ on the validation prior alone, which provided the largest single hand-tuned lift.

    \item \textbf{Classifier diversity $\gg$ segmentation diversity in ensembling, and automated search loops break when one parameter has asymmetric leverage.} Five kNN voters on the same segmentation contributed $+0.008$; three voters with diverse segmentation backends all regressed Kaggle. Separately, the autoresearch loop overfit the gate $\tau$ on a 5-FOV validation set within $\sim$10 iterations and inverted the local-Kaggle correlation. Once segmentation is good, commit to it and ensemble in classifier space; once a parameter has asymmetric metric leverage on a small validation set, automated search must re-anchor or split validation.
\end{enumerate}
